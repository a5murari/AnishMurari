OVERVIEW

Background & Methodology

This audit analyzes Nike's Instagram account (@nike) during a particularly active period for the brand — the lead-up to the 2026 FIFA World Cup. The 20 most recent posts as of June 23, 2026 were tracked, covering content published between June 2 and June 23.

For each post, four engagement metrics were logged: likes, comments, reposts, and shares. Posts were also categorized by content type (campaign-branded vs. athlete-driven vs. product) and tagged for the primary subject featured. The goal was to identify whether Nike's heavy investment in the "Rip The Script" World Cup campaign was translating into engagement, and which content archetypes were actually driving performance.

The sample period captured a natural experiment: Nike was running a coordinated, high-production World Cup campaign while simultaneously posting athlete-centric and product content. Comparing engagement across these content types against a single non-campaign post produced the audit's central finding.

CENTRAL FINDING

13M+

A single athlete photo — Ronaldo and LeBron captioned "Define Greatness. Then Redefine It." — generated more likes than every World Cup campaign post combined. It had zero campaign branding.

CONTENT STRATEGY

What Nike Was Posting

The 20-post sample broke down into three distinct content pillars. 

1. The first pillar was the World Cup Campaign Content, which were posts tied to Nike's "Rip The Script" campaign featuring a fictional narrative built around Erling Haaland and Channing Tatum, high-production football visuals, and pre-event teaser content. This accounted for roughly half the posts in the sample.

2. The second pillar was Athlete Moment Content, as there were posts celebrating big wins and milestones such as Jalen Brunson's NBA Finals MVP, Mirra Andreeva winning the French Open at 19, and  Nelly Korda's LPGA major.These posts were reactive, tied to live sporting events, and typically featured a clean athlete image with a punchy tagline.

3. The third pillar was Icon Content, featuring posts of Nike's biggest global athletes in a standalone context.This pillar had the smallest post count but produced the highest engagement numbers by a significant margin.

ENGAGEMENT ANALYSIS

Campaign vs. Icon Content

The "Rip The Script" campaign's best-performing post — "It was all going to plan until instincts took over" — generated 2.4M likes and 3.84M total engagement. That's a strong number in isolation, however compared to the Ronaldo/LeBron post (13M likes, 14.2M total engagement), the campaign's flagship content performed at roughly 27% of the top outlier's engagement, despite significantly higher production value and coordinated cross-account amplification.

The "Sleep well, NY" post — a single cinematic reel celebrating the Knicks' championship with no explicit product or campaign tie-in — generated 747K likes and 1.01M total engagement, making it the second highest-performing post by total engagement excluding the top outlier. Again, no campaign branding.

The weakest performers in the sample were all campaign-adjacent content with tactical framing: the Strikers Know Best series (48.2K likes), the Cantona short film teaser (16.2K likes), and the wheelchair tennis celebration post (22.3K likes). These posts carried stronger branded messaging but generated a fraction of the engagement produced by simpler, icon-led content.

KEY INSIGHTS

What the Data Shows

Insight 01

Icon pairings outperform campaign content by a wide margin

The Ronaldo × LeBron post generated 14.2M total engagement — more than 3.5x the next best post and more than every campaign post combined. The common factor: two globally recognized athletes, no campaign tag, no product push.

Insight 02

Cultural moments drive spikes, not campaign mechanics

The "Sleep well, NY" Knicks post (1.01M total eng.) and the Jalen Brunson Finals MVP post (348K) both outperformed most campaign content. They were reactive, tied to real-world events, and had minimal production complexity.

Insight 03

Campaign fatigue is visible in the data after week 2

Rip The Script posts published after June 13 averaged 56K total engagement, a steep drop from the campaign's opening posts (1.53M and 3.84M). Later campaign content underperformed even the non-campaign mid-sample posts.

Insight 04

Comments-to-likes ratio signals depth of engagement

The Ronaldo/LeBron post had a 0.48% comment-to-like ratio. The Vini Jr campaign post had a 5.0% ratio, which is higher, polarizing or conversation-driving content. High comment ratios don't always mean positive reception.

RECOMMENDATIONS

Strategic Takeaways for Nike

REC 01

Invest more in cross-sport, icon-pairing content

The data strongly supports scheduling 2–3 non-campaign athlete pairing posts per major event window. These require minimal production relative to their engagement return and can be seeded ahead of or during campaign pushes to sustain overall account performance.

REC 02

Shorten campaign content windows

The Rip The Script campaign ran over 3 weeks and showed clear engagement decay after week 1. Future campaigns should front-load the highest-concept content and compress the timeline, maintaining urgency and preventing audience drop-off.

REC 03

Build a reactive content pipeline alongside campaigns

The best-performing non-outlier posts were reactive (Knicks win, Brunson MVP, Andreeva French Open). Nike should maintain a real-time content team dedicated to athlete moments that can go live within hours of results, capitalizing on cultural conversation at its peak.

REC 04

Use comment ratio as a campaign health metric

Comments-to-likes ratio is a more sensitive signal of audience reaction than raw likes alone. Tracking this metric across campaign posts in real time would allow Nike's social team to identify underperforming or negatively-received content earlier and adjust mid-campaign.
